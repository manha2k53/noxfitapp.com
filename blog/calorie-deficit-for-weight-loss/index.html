Calorie Deficit for Weight Loss: How Big Should It Actually Be?

NF
NoxFit Editorial Team
June 20, 2026 · 12 min read · Reviewed by a Registered Dietitian

A calorie deficit for weight loss means consuming fewer calories than your body burns. The size of that deficit determines how fast you lose weight — and whether you lose fat or muscle. A deficit too small produces no results; a deficit too large causes muscle loss, metabolic slowdown, fatigue, and is almost impossible to sustain [1].

This guide explains exactly how to calculate your personal deficit, what size is optimal, and how to create it without feeling constantly hungry.

How a Calorie Deficit Works

Your body burns calories constantly — at rest (basal metabolic rate), during movement (activity), and digesting food (thermic effect of food). The total is your TDEE: Total Daily Energy Expenditure. When you eat fewer calories than your TDEE, your body turns to stored energy — primarily fat — to make up the difference.

One kilogram of body fat contains approximately 7,700 kcal of stored energy [1]. To lose 1 kg of fat, you need to create a cumulative deficit of 7,700 kcal. At 500 kcal/day deficit, that takes roughly 15 days. At 1,000 kcal/day, about 8 days — but a 1,000 kcal deficit is very aggressive and typically unsustainable.

💡 Key insight: The "3,500 calories = 1 lb of fat" rule (5,500 kcal = 1 kg) is an approximation. Real fat loss also depends on water retention, glycogen levels, and metabolic adaptation. The scale does not always reflect true fat loss week to week.

A 500 kcal/day deficit is the research-backed sweet spot for most people. It produces consistent fat loss (0.5 kg/week), preserves muscle mass when protein is adequate, and is sustainable for months without significant metabolic adaptation [2].

⚠️ Never go below 1,200 kcal/day (women) or 1,500 kcal/day (men) without medical supervision. Eating too little triggers metabolic adaptation — your body reduces its calorie burn to match intake — making further fat loss extremely difficult and causing significant muscle loss.

How to Create a Calorie Deficit Without Starving

The most sustainable deficit combines moderate food reduction with increased activity — rather than cutting calories alone. A 500 kcal/day deficit achieved by cutting 250 kcal from food and burning 250 kcal extra through exercise is far easier to sustain than cutting 500 kcal from food alone.

  • Cut liquid calories first. Soft drinks, juice, alcohol, and flavoured coffee can add 300–600 kcal/day without contributing to fullness. Eliminating these alone often creates the entire deficit without changing any food.
  • Increase protein to 1.6–2.2g/kg/day. Protein is the most satiating macronutrient — it reduces hunger better than carbs or fat at the same calorie level, making the deficit easier to sustain.
  • Add 3,000–5,000 steps/day. This burns roughly 100–200 kcal extra with zero impact on hunger or recovery. Walking is the highest-return activity for creating a painless calorie deficit.
  • Eat more volume-dense, low-calorie foods. Vegetables, broth-based soups, and whole fruit provide physical fullness for fewer calories. A 500g salad (100 kcal) takes up more stomach space than a 500 kcal bag of crisps.
  • Don't create the deficit through exercise alone. A 45-minute run burns ~350 kcal — but often increases appetite by a similar amount. Exercise is excellent for health and metabolic rate, but diet provides more reliable control over the deficit.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many calories should I eat to lose 1 kg per week? +
To lose 1 kg/week you need a 1,000 kcal/day deficit (7,000 kcal/week ÷ 7 days). For most people this means eating 1,200–1,600 kcal/day — which is possible but difficult to sustain and carries risk of muscle loss without high protein intake. Most people do better targeting 0.5 kg/week (500 kcal/day deficit) for sustainable results.
Will I lose muscle on a calorie deficit? +
You will lose some muscle on any calorie deficit — this is unavoidable. The amount depends on deficit size, protein intake, and whether you resistance train. A 500 kcal/day deficit with 1.6–2.2g/kg protein and strength training 2–3× per week minimises muscle loss to nearly zero for most people. Larger deficits and low protein intake significantly increase muscle loss.
Why am I not losing weight in a calorie deficit? +
The most common reason is underestimating calories eaten. Research consistently shows people underreport calorie intake by 20–50%. A tablespoon of peanut butter is 90 kcal; a splash of olive oil is 60 kcal. These add up. Weigh your food with a scale for 2 weeks to get an accurate picture. Other causes: water retention masking fat loss, inaccurate TDEE estimates, or hormonal factors worth discussing with a doctor if the deficit is confirmed and weight still does not move.
Is it OK to have a larger deficit on some days? +
Yes — what matters is your average weekly deficit, not the daily number. Eating at maintenance one day and 1,000 kcal deficit the next averages to a 500 kcal/day deficit across two days. This is the basis of "calorie cycling" and works well for people who prefer larger deficits during the week and more flexibility on weekends.
