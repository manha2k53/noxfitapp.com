How to Count Calories for Weight Loss: Beginner's Complete Guide

NF
NoxFit Editorial Team
June 24, 2026 · 11 min read · Reviewed by a Registered Dietitian

Counting calories is the most reliable method for creating and verifying a calorie deficit — the only non-negotiable requirement for fat loss. It's not the most glamorous approach, but a 2019 meta-analysis found calorie tracking is one of the single strongest predictors of successful weight loss and maintenance [1]. This guide walks through the exact process: how to find your target, how to weigh food accurately, how to read labels, and the 5 mistakes that cause counting to fail.

Step 1: Find Your Calorie Target

Before tracking anything, you need a daily calorie target. This is your TDEE (Total Daily Energy Expenditure) minus your desired deficit:

1
Calculate your TDEE
Use the Mifflin-St Jeor equation or an online calculator. Enter your weight, height, age, and activity level. Your TDEE is the number of calories that maintains your current weight. Use our free calculator →
2
Subtract 300–500 kcal for your daily target
A 500 kcal/day deficit produces ~0.5 kg/week of fat loss. A 300 kcal deficit is more sustainable long-term. Deficits over 750 kcal/day increase muscle loss and are hard to maintain. Start with 400–500 kcal.
3
Set protein first
Aim for 1.6–2.2g protein per kg bodyweight. Fill the remaining calories with carbs and fat to your preference. Protein is the most important macro for fat loss — don't leave it as an afterthought. Full protein guide →
💡 Example: 80 kg person, sedentary office job → TDEE ~2,200 kcal → Target: 1,700 kcal (500 kcal deficit) → Protein target: 144g (1.8g/kg) → Remaining 1,124 kcal for carbs and fat.

Step 2: Log Every Meal (Including the Small Stuff)

The biggest mistake people make is tracking main meals but not condiments, cooking oils, drinks, and "tastes" while cooking. These add up to 200–400 hidden calories for most people.

What you must log: Every meal, snack, drink (other than water), cooking oil, sauces, dressings, and condiments. A tablespoon of olive oil is 120 kcal. A drizzle of ketchup is 15 kcal. A "small" handful of almonds while cooking is 150 kcal.

When to log: Log food before or immediately after eating — not at the end of the day when memory is unreliable. Research shows same-time logging is 30–40% more accurate than retrospective logging [2].

Step 3: Weigh Your Food — Don't Eyeball

Eyeballing portion sizes is the #1 reason calorie counting fails. Studies consistently show people underestimate food portions by 20–50% when using visual estimation. A "medium" chicken breast in someone's hand can range from 120g (180 kcal) to 250g (370 kcal).

How to weigh food accurately:

  • Place your plate or bowl on the food scale and press TARE (zero) before adding each food
  • Weigh raw meat and fish (cooking changes weight via water loss — always weigh raw and use raw nutrition data)
  • Weigh dry oats, rice, and pasta (cooked weight varies widely by water absorption)
  • Weigh cooking oil in the pan before heating — you can't measure it after
  • A £10 digital food scale is one of the highest-ROI purchases for weight loss
⚠️ Raw vs cooked: 100g raw chicken breast ≈ 165 kcal. 100g cooked chicken breast ≈ 220 kcal (same chicken, less water). Always log the state you're weighing in (raw or cooked) and match it to the correct database entry.

5 Common Calorie Counting Mistakes

❌ Mistake 1: Not logging cooking oil
A generous pour of olive oil when stir-frying is 2–3 tablespoons = 240–360 kcal added to an otherwise low-calorie meal. Measure oil with a measuring spoon or use a cooking spray (5 kcal vs 120+ kcal per tablespoon).
❌ Mistake 2: Choosing the wrong database entry
"Chicken breast" in a food app might have 30+ entries. "Grilled chicken breast" and "raw chicken breast" have different calorie counts. Always choose the entry that matches how you're weighing it (raw or cooked) and from a verified source like USDA data.
❌ Mistake 3: Not logging drinks
A large latte (350 kcal), a glass of wine (150 kcal), and a juice (140 kcal) add 640 kcal — almost half a day's food for many people in deficit. Log every drink except plain water and plain tea/coffee.
❌ Mistake 4: Quitting after one bad day
Missing a day of tracking doesn't ruin your progress. Start logging again the next morning. Weight loss happens over weeks, not days. One overeating day sets you back at most 2–3 days of progress — not the whole effort.
❌ Mistake 5: Trusting gym machine calorie counters
Cardio machines overestimate calorie burn by 20–90%. A treadmill might say you burned 400 kcal when you burned 230. Don't "eat back" exercise calories from machine estimates — they're unreliable. Use a modest 10–20% of estimated exercise calories as a conservative adjustment.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many calories should I eat to lose weight? +
It depends on your TDEE (Total Daily Energy Expenditure). A general starting point is 1,500–1,800 kcal/day for most women and 1,800–2,200 kcal/day for most men aiming for fat loss, but individual variation is significant. Calculate your specific TDEE using a Mifflin-St Jeor calculator and subtract 400–500 kcal for a sustainable deficit. Never go below 1,200 kcal (women) or 1,400 kcal (men) without medical supervision.
Do I need to count calories forever? +
No. Most people track consistently for 3–6 months, then periodically check in (e.g. 1 week per month) to prevent drift. After tracking accurately for several months, most people develop a reliable intuitive sense of portion sizes and calorie density. Tracking is a teaching tool, not a lifelong obligation — though many people continue because they find it useful for accountability.
Is calorie counting accurate enough to work? +
Calorie counts on labels are allowed to be off by ±20% in most countries, and metabolic rate estimates can vary ±10–15%. So your "1,700 kcal deficit day" might actually be 1,500–1,900 kcal. This is why results should be judged by the scale over 4 weeks, not by daily calculations. If you're not losing weight after 3 weeks of consistent tracking, reduce your target by 100–150 kcal rather than assuming the method doesn't work.
