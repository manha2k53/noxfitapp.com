What Is Body Recomposition?

Body recomposition (or "recomp") means losing fat and gaining muscle simultaneously — changing your body composition without necessarily changing your weight much on the scale. Your body weight might stay the same or drop slightly, but you get leaner and more muscular at the same time.

For years, fitness culture insisted this was impossible — that you had to choose between a "bulk" (calorie surplus to gain muscle) or a "cut" (calorie deficit to lose fat). But a growing body of research shows this is a false binary, especially for certain populations.

Key insight: Muscle can be synthesized using stored body fat as energy, not just dietary calories. This is why recomposition is physiologically possible — your fat stores provide the fuel, while dietary protein provides the building blocks.

Who Can Do Body Recomposition?

Recomp works best for some people and is nearly impossible for others. Here's an honest breakdown:

✅ Best candidates

Beginners (<1 year training)

"Newbie gains" — the initial muscle growth response is so strong that beginners build muscle even in a deficit. The most pronounced recomp window of your life.

✅ Good candidates

People returning after a break

Muscle memory allows faster regain of lost muscle. Fat stores from the detraining period provide energy for rebuilding.

✅ Good candidates

Overweight/higher body fat (%)

More fat stores available as fuel. Research shows recomp rate correlates with body fat percentage — more fat = more fuel available for muscle synthesis.

⚠️ Harder

Advanced, lean trainees

Near their genetic ceiling for muscle. Minimal fat stores as fuel. For these individuals, traditional bulk/cut cycles are more efficient.

The Calorie Strategy for Recomposition

This is where recomp differs from a standard cut. You don't want an aggressive deficit — you want to eat near maintenance or at a very small deficit (100–200 calories below TDEE).

Why? A large deficit (500+ kcal) signals to your body that resources are scarce, suppressing muscle protein synthesis. A small deficit allows muscle building to continue while still using stored fat for the energy gap.

GoalCalorie TargetExpected Result
Pure fat loss (cut)TDEE − 400–600 kcalFast fat loss, some muscle loss risk
Body recompositionTDEE − 100–200 kcalSlow fat loss + simultaneous muscle gain
Muscle gain (bulk)TDEE + 200–400 kcalFast muscle gain + some fat gain

The trade-off: recomp is slower than either a dedicated bulk or cut. Expect 0.5–1kg of fat loss per month — not per week. The payoff is that you're improving body composition in both directions simultaneously.

Protein: The Non-Negotiable

Protein is the single most important variable in body recomposition — more important than calorie timing, training split, or supplements. Here's why: muscle protein synthesis (the process of building new muscle) requires a constant supply of amino acids. Without enough protein, your body simply cannot build muscle regardless of how hard you train.

For recomposition, research by Barakat et al. (2020) suggests 2.2–2.4g protein per kg of bodyweight — higher than standard muscle-gain recommendations — because the slight calorie deficit increases protein oxidation, so you need more to maintain a positive nitrogen balance.

Training Plan for Body Recomposition

Strength training is essential — cardio alone won't cause muscle growth. The optimal recomp training approach:

  • 3–4 days/week full-body or upper/lower split — frequency matters more than volume for recomp
  • 6–20 reps per set, close to failure — hypertrophy range with sufficient intensity
  • Progressive overload every 1–2 weeks — add weight or reps consistently
  • Compound movements first — squats, deadlifts, bench, rows, overhead press
  • Add 2–3 low-intensity cardio sessions — walking, cycling — to burn additional fat without impairing recovery

How to Track Recomposition Progress

This is where most people get frustrated — the scale barely moves during recomp. You must use multiple tracking methods:

  • Scale weight — expect slow change or no change. This is normal and expected.
  • Progress photos — take monthly front, side, and back photos in consistent lighting. Visual change is often dramatic even when scale weight is flat.
  • Body measurements — waist, hips, chest, arms, thighs. Waist shrinking + arm growing = recomp working.
  • Strength gains — if you're lifting heavier over time, you're building muscle. Simple but reliable.
  • How clothes fit — pants looser at the waist, shirts tighter at the shoulders = textbook recomp.
The mindset shift: Stop weighing yourself daily. Monthly check-ins with photos and measurements tell you far more than daily scale fluctuations. The scale is a terrible tool for tracking recomposition — use it as one data point, not the only one.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does body recomposition take?
Expect 3–6 months before clear visual changes. Recomp is slower than a dedicated cut or bulk — typically 0.5–1kg of fat loss per month alongside 0.5–1kg of muscle gain per month. After 6 months, the physical change can be striking even if the scale barely moved.
Should I eat more on training days?
Nutrient timing around workouts is less important than total daily intake. However, eating most of your carbohydrates around your workout (pre and post) can improve performance and recovery during recomp. Protein should be spread evenly across 3–5 meals throughout the day.
Is body recomposition better than bulking and cutting?
For beginners and intermediate trainees with higher body fat, recomp is often better — you improve aesthetics in both directions simultaneously without the mental difficulty of watching the scale go up during a bulk. For advanced lean athletes, traditional bulk/cut cycles are more efficient for maximizing muscle growth.
Do I need supplements for body recomposition?
No supplements are required. Creatine monohydrate (5g/day) has the strongest evidence for improving strength and muscle retention during recomp and is inexpensive. Protein powder can help hit your daily protein target. Everything else is largely unnecessary.
