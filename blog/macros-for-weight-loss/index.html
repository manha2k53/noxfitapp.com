Macros for Weight Loss: How to Calculate & Hit Your Targets

NF
NoxFit Editorial Team
June 21, 2026 · 11 min read · Reviewed by a Registered Dietitian

Counting macros for weight loss — protein, carbohydrates, and fat — gives you more precise control over your diet than calorie counting alone. While a calorie deficit is the non-negotiable foundation of fat loss, the macro split determines how much of that weight comes from fat versus muscle, how hungry you feel, and how much energy you have. Get it right and weight loss feels manageable. Get it wrong and you lose muscle, feel exhausted, and give up.

What Are Macros?

Macronutrients are the three main categories of food that provide energy. Every food you eat is a combination of these three, in different proportions:

  • Protein — 4 kcal/gram. Builds and repairs muscle, keeps you full, and has the highest thermic effect of food (20–30% of calories burned in digestion).
  • Carbohydrates — 4 kcal/gram. Primary fuel for the brain and high-intensity exercise. Stored as glycogen in muscles and liver.
  • Fat — 9 kcal/gram. Essential for hormones, fat-soluble vitamins (A/D/E/K), and brain function. The most calorie-dense macro.

Alcohol is sometimes called a "4th macro" at 7 kcal/gram — but it has no nutritional value and is metabolised before fat, effectively halting fat burning while present in the system.

Best Macro Split for Weight Loss

Research consistently shows that high protein intake is the most important macro variable for weight loss. A high-protein diet preserves muscle during a deficit, reduces hunger by 25–30%, and increases metabolic rate through the thermic effect of food [1]. The carb/fat split matters less than the protein target.

Deep Dive: Each Macro's Role in Weight Loss

Protein (30–40% of calories): The most important macro for weight loss. High protein intake (1.6–2.4g/kg/day) during a calorie deficit preserves lean muscle mass — which keeps your metabolic rate up, ensures you're losing fat rather than muscle, and maintains strength. Protein also has the highest satiety per calorie, reducing hunger better than carbs or fat [1]. Best sources: chicken breast, Greek yogurt, eggs, cottage cheese, fish, tofu, legumes.

Carbohydrates (30–40% of calories): Carbs are not the enemy — the type matters more than the amount. Refined carbs (white bread, sugar, juice) spike blood sugar and drive fat storage. Complex carbs (oats, sweet potato, brown rice, vegetables) digest slowly, provide sustained energy, and support gut health through fibre. Don't go below 100g/day without a specific reason — the brain requires ~130g glucose daily [3].

Fat (20–35% of calories): Don't go below 0.5g/kg/day — fat is essential for hormone production including testosterone and estrogen. Too little fat on a diet causes hormonal disruption, poor mood, and impaired absorption of vitamins A, D, E, and K. Prioritise unsaturated fats (olive oil, avocado, nuts, fish) over saturated fats from processed foods.

Research comparing different macro splits consistently finds that total calorie intake and protein content predict weight loss outcomes — not the carb/fat ratio. A meta-analysis of 48 studies found no significant difference in fat loss between low-fat and low-carb diets when protein and calories were matched [2].

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the best macro ratio for weight loss? +
The most evidence-backed ratio is 30–35% protein, 35–40% carbs, 25–30% fat. However, the research shows that total calories and protein intake matter far more than the carb/fat ratio. If you hit your protein target (1.6–2.2g/kg) and maintain a calorie deficit, you can distribute the remaining calories between carbs and fat based on personal preference and still lose fat effectively.
Should I count macros or just calories for weight loss? +
Counting macros is more effective than counting calories alone because it ensures you hit your protein target — which protects muscle mass and reduces hunger. However, if tracking macros feels overwhelming, starting with calories + a protein target (just two numbers) gives you most of the benefit with less complexity. You can add carb and fat tracking later.
How many grams of protein should I eat per day to lose weight? +
1.6–2.4g per kg of bodyweight per day. For a 70 kg person, that's 112–168g of protein daily. Aim for the higher end (2.0–2.4g/kg) if you're training hard or have a larger calorie deficit, as more protein is needed to protect muscle when eating less. Spread it across 3–5 meals for best muscle protein synthesis.
Is it bad to eat low fat while dieting? +
Going too low on fat (below 0.5g/kg/day) is harmful — it disrupts hormone production including testosterone and oestrogen, impairs absorption of vitamins A, D, E, and K, and can cause gallstone formation. Minimum fat intake during a deficit should be 40–50g/day for most people. Fat doesn't make you fat — excess calories do.
