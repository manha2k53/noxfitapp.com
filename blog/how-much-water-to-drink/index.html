The "8 Glasses a Day" Myth

The "8×8 rule" (eight 8-ounce glasses = ~2 litres/day) has been repeated so often it feels like medical fact. It isn't. A 2002 review by Dr. Heinz Valtin in the American Journal of Physiology found no scientific evidence supporting this specific recommendation — it likely originated from a misreading of a 1945 US food and nutrition board suggestion.

Your actual water needs depend on your body weight, activity level, climate, diet (food provides 20–30% of daily water needs), and whether you're pregnant or breastfeeding. One number fits nobody.

Your Personal Daily Water Target

The most evidence-backed formula: 35ml per kg of bodyweight per day, adjusted for activity and climate. Use the calculator below:

Water and Weight Loss: What the Research Shows

Water genuinely helps with weight loss through several mechanisms — not just because it's calorie-free:

  • Pre-meal water reduces calorie intake. A 2010 RCT (Dennis et al.) found drinking 500ml of water 30 minutes before each meal reduced calorie intake by 13% and produced 44% more weight loss over 12 weeks vs the control group.
  • Temporary metabolism boost. Drinking 500ml of cold water increases metabolic rate by ~30% for 30–40 minutes (Boschmann 2003). This is real but modest — roughly 24 extra calories burned per 500ml.
  • Reduces false hunger. Mild dehydration (1–2% body weight loss) is frequently misinterpreted as hunger. Drinking water first before reaching for a snack eliminates many unnecessary calories.
  • Replaces caloric drinks. Switching from soda, juice, or sweetened coffee to water removes 150–400 calories per day for most people — a massive difference over weeks.
Easiest weight loss hack: Drink 500ml of water before each meal, every day. A 2010 RCT found this alone produced 2 kg more weight loss over 12 weeks than dieting without pre-meal water. Zero cost, zero effort.

Urine colour is the most reliable real-time hydration indicator:

Other signs of dehydration: persistent headache, fatigue despite adequate sleep, difficulty concentrating, dry mouth, dizziness, and — most commonly — feeling hungry 30–60 minutes after a meal.

8 Tips to Drink More Water

  • Drink 500ml immediately after waking. You lose water overnight through breathing and sweat. Starting hydrated sets up the day.
  • Carry a 1-litre bottle and refill it twice. Removes the mental overhead of tracking glasses.
  • Set hourly phone reminders until drinking water becomes habitual (usually 3–4 weeks).
  • Eat water-rich foods. Cucumbers (96% water), watermelon (92%), strawberries (91%), and spinach (93%) contribute meaningfully to daily hydration.
  • Drink before every meal. Builds a permanent habit and reduces calorie intake simultaneously.
  • Add flavour naturally. Lemon, cucumber slices, or mint make plain water more appealing if you find it boring.
  • Drink before, during, and after exercise. Aim for 150–250ml every 15–20 minutes during workouts in warm conditions.
  • Track it for 2 weeks. Most people dramatically overestimate how much they drink. NoxFit's tracking feature can help.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does coffee and tea count toward daily water intake?
Yes — contrary to popular belief, caffeinated drinks do contribute to hydration. A 2014 study (Killer et al.) found coffee was just as hydrating as water in regular coffee drinkers. The mild diuretic effect of caffeine is offset by the volume of liquid consumed. Herbal teas are fully hydrating.
Can you drink too much water?
Yes — hyponatremia (water intoxication) occurs when you drink so much water that sodium levels become dangerously diluted. This is rare in normal life but happens to endurance athletes who drink plain water for hours without electrolytes. For most people, your kidneys can handle up to 1 litre/hour — don't drink more than this.
Does drinking cold water burn more calories?
Very slightly — your body expends energy warming cold water to body temperature. The effect is roughly 8 extra calories per 250ml glass of ice water. Over a full day of cold water drinking, this might add up to 30–50 extra calories burned. Real, but negligible compared to diet and exercise.
Why do I still feel thirsty after drinking water?
Persistent thirst despite drinking can indicate: sodium deficiency (electrolyte imbalance from sweating), high sugar or sodium intake driving water retention, or in rare cases, conditions like diabetes insipidus. If persistent, check your electrolyte intake first — add a pinch of salt or electrolyte supplement to your water.
