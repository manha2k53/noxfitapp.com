How Many Calories to Lose Weight? (Science + Free Calculator)

NF
NoxFit Editorial Team
June 16, 2026 · 12 min read · Reviewed by a Registered Dietitian

The answer to "how many calories to lose weight" is not a single number — it's a formula based on your body, your goals, and how fast you want to lose. The good news: the science is well-established and the calculation takes under 60 seconds.

This guide explains BMR, TDEE, and the right deficit to create — with a working calculator you can use right now. No guesswork. No extremes.

Step 1: Calculate Your BMR (Basal Metabolic Rate)

Your BMR is the number of calories your body burns at complete rest — just to keep organs functioning, blood circulating, and breathing going. It accounts for 60–70% of your total daily calorie burn [1].

The most accurate formula for non-athletes is the Mifflin-St Jeor equation, validated in a 2005 review as the most precise for healthy adults [2]:

Men: BMR = (10 × weight in kg) + (6.25 × height in cm) − (5 × age) + 5
Women: BMR = (10 × weight in kg) + (6.25 × height in cm) − (5 × age) − 161

For example, a 30-year-old woman who weighs 70 kg and is 165 cm tall has a BMR of: (10 × 70) + (6.25 × 165) − (5 × 30) − 161 = 1,445 kcal/day.

Step 2: Find Your TDEE (Total Daily Energy Expenditure)

Your BMR only tells you the bare minimum. Your TDEE is what you actually burn each day, accounting for movement, exercise, and the energy used to digest food [1]. Multiply your BMR by your activity factor:

Activity LevelDescriptionMultiplier
SedentaryOffice job, little exercise× 1.2
Lightly ActiveLight exercise 1–3 days/week× 1.375
Moderately ActiveModerate exercise 3–5 days/week× 1.55
Very ActiveHard exercise 6–7 days/week× 1.725
Extra ActivePhysical job + daily training× 1.9

So for our example: 1,445 × 1.375 (lightly active) = 1,987 kcal/day TDEE. This is her maintenance — how much she needs to eat to stay at her current weight.

💡 Important: Most people overestimate their activity level. If you have a desk job and walk casually, you are sedentary (×1.2), not lightly active. Overestimating leads to eating too much and stalled weight loss [3].

How Big Should Your Calorie Deficit Be?

Not all deficits are equal. The research consistently points to a moderate deficit of 300–500 kcal/day as the safest and most sustainable approach for most adults [4]:

  • Too small (under 200 kcal): Weight loss is extremely slow and easily wiped out by minor daily fluctuations in water retention.
  • Moderate (300–500 kcal): Produces 0.3–0.5 kg/week of fat loss. Muscle mass is preserved. Hunger is manageable. Adherence is high.
  • Too large (750+ kcal): Faster scale movement, but significantly more muscle loss, hormonal disruption, and risk of metabolic adaptation — your body slows its burn to compensate [5].

3 Common Calorie Mistakes That Stall Weight Loss

  1. Underreporting portion sizes. A 2020 study found that people underestimate calorie intake by 30–50% on average [3]. Measure your food for 7 days — you will be surprised how much a "handful" actually weighs.
  2. Not accounting for exercise calories burned. Many people eat back all of their exercise calories based on gym machine estimates, which overstate calorie burn by 25–40% [7]. Use your activity multiplier in TDEE instead of adding calories back.
  3. Setting too aggressive a deficit and quitting. A 1,000 kcal daily deficit means extreme hunger, muscle loss, and fatigue. Studies consistently show that adherence drops sharply below 1,200 kcal in women and 1,500 kcal in men [4]. Slow and steady wins.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many calories should I eat to lose 1kg per week? +
To lose 1kg per week you need a daily deficit of approximately 1,100 kcal (7,700 ÷ 7). For most people this means eating below 1,200 kcal, which is generally not recommended without medical supervision. A more sustainable approach is targeting 0.5 kg/week with a 550 kcal deficit.
Is it true that eating below 1,200 calories puts your body in starvation mode? +
"Starvation mode" is a simplification of a real phenomenon: metabolic adaptation. When you severely restrict calories, your body reduces its resting burn by up to 15% [5]. This is a real risk below ~1,200 kcal for women and ~1,500 for men, but it doesn't switch on and off suddenly — it's a gradual downward adjustment.
Should I eat back the calories I burn during exercise? +
Not if you calculated your TDEE using an activity multiplier — that already accounts for exercise. If you used a sedentary multiplier and then exercise, add back 50% of estimated exercise calories (not 100%, since machine estimates are typically 25–40% too high).
Why am I not losing weight despite eating in a deficit? +
Most commonly: underreporting (portion sizes are bigger than estimated), metabolic adaptation (body adjusting), or water retention masking fat loss on the scale. Take monthly waist measurements alongside scale weight — they tell a more accurate story than daily weigh-ins.
